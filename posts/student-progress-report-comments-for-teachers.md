Progress report season arrives twice a year like a dentist appointment you forgot to cancel. You know it's coming. You're still not ready.

The comments are the hardest part. Not because teachers don't know their students — you know them better than anyone — but because translating six weeks of observations into three sentences that are honest, professional, and specific is genuinely hard. Especially times 25.

Here's what helps: examples to get you started, and a workflow that means you're not starting from scratch.

---

## What makes a strong progress report comment

Before the examples — a quick framework. The best progress report comments do three things:

1. **Name something specific.** Not "works hard" — what does that mean? "Completes independent work without reminders" is specific. "Has shown growth in managing transitions" is specific. Generic comments protect no one and tell parents nothing.

2. **Balance honesty with forward momentum.** You can be truthful about a struggle without making it feel like a verdict. "Is working on" and "with continued support" are not hedging — they're accurate and they keep the door open.

3. **Connect to something you actually observed.** This is the one most teachers skip because they don't have notes. If your comment could apply to any student in your class, it probably should be rewritten.

## Progress report comment examples by category

### Academic progress — strong

- "[Name] is performing above grade-level expectations in [subject] and demonstrates a clear understanding of [skill]."
- "[Name] applies new concepts quickly and is ready for extension work in [area]."
- "[Name] consistently demonstrates mastery of grade-level skills and contributes meaningfully to class discussions."
- "[Name] reads with strong fluency and comprehension. Their ability to make connections across texts is a standout strength."
- "[Name] approaches math with confidence and accuracy. Problem-solving is a particular strength."

### Academic progress — on track

- "[Name] is meeting grade-level expectations in all areas and making steady progress."
- "[Name] is developing grade-appropriate skills in [subject] and benefits from continued practice at home."
- "[Name] is working at grade level and shows growing confidence in [area]."
- "[Name] demonstrates solid understanding of core concepts. Applying them independently is the next step."
- "[Name] is on track. Continued reading at home will support the growth we're seeing."

### Academic progress — area of concern

- "[Name] is working below grade-level expectations in [subject]. We are providing additional support and monitoring progress closely."
- "[Name] has shown some difficulty with [specific skill] this term. We are addressing this with targeted strategies in class."
- "[Name] would benefit from additional practice in [area]. I'd welcome a conversation about how we can support this at home."
- "[Name] is making progress, though [skill] remains challenging. We will continue to focus on this next term."
- "I want to flag [area] as something to watch. [Name] is receiving support and I'll keep you updated on how they respond."

### Effort and work habits

- "[Name] is a strong self-starter who manages their time well and rarely needs reminders to stay on task."
- "[Name] puts in consistent effort and approaches challenges with a positive attitude."
- "[Name] is working on building stronger work habits. With support and structure, they are making progress."
- "[Name] benefits from frequent check-ins during independent work. We are building the stamina for longer tasks."
- "[Name] shows more effort in [context] than in [context]. We are working on transferring that motivation across all subjects."
- "[Name] completes work to a high standard when focused. Consistency is the goal for next term."

### Social and emotional development

- "[Name] is a kind and cooperative member of our classroom community."
- "[Name] navigates peer relationships with maturity and handles conflict calmly."
- "[Name] is developing confidence in group settings and is becoming more willing to take academic risks."
- "[Name] is working on self-regulation strategies and has shown real growth since September."
- "Unstructured time continues to be a challenge for [Name]. We are working on giving them tools to navigate it independently."
- "[Name] benefits from adult support during transitions. This is an area of focus for next term."

### Special education / IEP-aligned

Keep these factual, observable, and tied to documented goals.

- "[Name] is making progress toward their IEP goals in [area]. We will review at the next team meeting."
- "[Name] is working with the support team on [goal] and is responding well to [strategy]."
- "With the accommodations in place, [Name] is accessing grade-level content and making meaningful gains."
- "[Name]'s progress in [area] is documented and will be discussed at our upcoming meeting. I look forward to connecting with the family."

## The blank-page problem — and how to fix it

The reason progress report comments take so long isn't the writing. It's the remembering.

You sit down in November and try to reconstruct six weeks of observations from memory. You check your gradebook. You flip through a notebook. You think, "I know something happened with this student in October but I can't remember what." You write something vague because vague is safe when you don't have specifics.

That's the problem ShortHand was built to solve.

When you log student notes in real time — even 10 seconds after class while things are fresh — that data is waiting for you when progress report season arrives. You're not reconstructing. You're reviewing. And reviewing is fast.

ShortHand's AI reporting takes your logged notes and generates a draft progress comment for each student based on what you actually observed. Not a template with [Name] swapped in. A comment that reflects the real pattern: the student who struggled in October but turned it around in November, the student who does great in math and consistently needs support during writing, the student you called home about twice.

The draft isn't always perfect — you still read it, adjust it, make it yours. But starting from a draft that knows the student is a completely different experience than starting from a blank cursor.
